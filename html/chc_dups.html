DUPLICATE HERBARIUM SPECIMENS

In the context of botanical specimens, duplicates are multiple specimens of what is thought to be the same taxon that are stored as separate accessions or distributed separately. They are usually collected at the same time by the same person and bear the same collection number. But sometimes, as in exsiccatae sets, they are collected at multiple times and places but are considered by the original distributor as examples of the same taxon; they may only be linked by their exsiccatae name and number.

The fate of duplicates is to be distributed in the process of exchange to multiple institutions (sometimes an institution has multiple duplicates of a specimen). The identification of individuals of a set of duplicates often diverges as each is examined by a different suite of users.

Curators at one institution try to take advantage of identifications made at other institutions by using annotations with the rubric "duplicate at ... determined as ..."

The consolidated data view of the Consortium of California Herbaria provides an interesting exposition of the problems of recognizing duplicates, and the difficulties involved in trying to keep duplicates in sync.

I extracted one class of "duplicates" from the 850,000 CCH specimens. I chose those specimens collected on the same date bearing the same collection number by the same collector last name that bear a different determination. There are 19,470 sets (two or more specimens, one of which bears a different name from the rest) of these possible duplicates. They are not all duplicates: some are the result of false positives, as you will see below. This represents about 5% of all specimens. For comparison, about 16% of the specimens participate in the set of relatively certain duplicates (same date, same number, same taxon name). So about 80% of the specimens in the Consortium are represented at only one herbarium.

I did not use the complete collector's name, because that is often represented differently in different databases: in fact, I left it out in the first version of the extract.

Note that duplicate specimens for which there is no collector number or for which there is no single day date are not represented.

    • JEPS15148, A. E. Wieslander = Castilleja exserta subsp. exserta UC569920, A. E. Wieslander = Castilleja exserta show dups
    This is fairly straightforward: a minor difference in assignment to infraspecific category.

    JEPS13838, Willis L. Jepson = Salix lasiolepis JEPS38397, Willis L. Jepson = Salix laevigata show dups
    It's not possible to know the explanation of this "duplicate" without looking at the specimens. Was the collection number correctly entered? Was one of the specimens misidentified? Was the collection originally mixed?

    JEPS18987, Carl V. Meyer = Gutierrezia sarothrae UC489049, C. V. Meyer = Gutierrezia californica show dups
    This shows the value of including annotation history (or having an image). If you follow the links to the detail page, you will see that both specimens were collected as G. sarothae and that Meredith Lane confirmed the identification of one and redetermined the other as G. californica in 1983. More investigation is required to determine what kind of error has occurred. This also shows why you can't easily use the collector's name to extract duplicates.

    JEPS20002, W. L. Jepson = Fimbristylis vahlii JEPS44326, W. L. Jepson = Ludwigia peploides subsp. peploides show dups
    Did Jepson unintentionally use the same number? Was there a typo on the label? Was the label misentered? More implausible: was the taxon name or the collector misentered?

    JEPS101336, Dean Wm. Taylor = Senecio pauciflorus JEPS101337, Dean Wm. Taylor = Packera pauciflora show dups
    In the same herbarium, nomenclatural synonyms haven't been synchronized. In JEPS, there is a separate synoptical "reference collection" that is sometimes a backwater.

    JEPS1758, Milo S. Baker = Balsamorhiza hirsuta UC964788, Milo S. Baker = Balsamorhiza macrolepis var. platylepis RSA74324, Milo S. Baker = Balsamorhiza hookeri var. lanata show dups
    This doesn't happen often: three herbaria, three duplicates, three different names
